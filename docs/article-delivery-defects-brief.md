# Tesla delivery defects — research brief

Internal research brief for the article "Tesla delivery defects — what to do when you find one." Prose here flows into the published page; quotes are preserved verbatim with source URLs so the writer can drop them in without re-checking.

## Methodology

**Primary sources.** Owner forums where delivery experiences are narrated in full, with follow-ups and outcomes:
- teslamotorsclub.com (TMC) — largest archive of delivery threads, US- and UK-heavy
- teslaownersonline.com (TOO) — smaller, often more thoughtful long-form posts
- cybertruckownersclub.com (CTOC) — recent (2024–2026) deliveries, useful for current Tesla policy patterns
- Tesla's own support pages (tesla.com/support/taking-delivery, /delivery-day, /after-taking-delivery) for official policy framing

**Search strategy.** Site-scoped queries on the four prioritized forums plus tesla.com, focused on: refusing delivery, due bills, post-delivery defect windows ("100 miles," "24 hours," "72 hours," "3 days"), executive escalation, panel gap tolerance, and outcome threads where the original poster updated.

**Date range.** Threads from roughly 2018 to early 2026. Recent threads (2024+) on CTOC and TMC weighted higher because Tesla's delivery process has changed materially: deliveries are now app-driven, advisors are scarce on lots, and "self-pickup" is common. Older 2018–2020 threads are still useful for the human dynamics (refusing, due bills, escalation), but their procedural specifics may be stale.

## Topic 1: Refusing delivery vs accepting with issues

### Owner-voice positions

The dominant forum position, repeated across hundreds of threads, is: **refuse if anything is more than minor, because once you sign, leverage collapses.** A countervailing thread of voices says refusal is overrated for cosmetic-only issues, because (a) the next VIN is not guaranteed to be better, and (b) Tesla has gotten faster at honoring due bills for clear paint/trim issues.

### Decision framework

**Refuse when:**
- Damage is structural or safety-related (steering fault on screen at handover, frame/subframe damage, missing hardware on a seat or seatbelt anchor).
- A defect requires repaint of a large panel — paint-shop work post-delivery is widely reported as a degraded experience versus factory paint.
- Multiple unrelated defects (the 5+ issue case). Each one individually might be fixable; collectively they suggest a unit that wasn't QC'd.
- Anything Tesla can later argue you caused: chips on a leading edge, scuffed wheels, cracked glass.

**Accept with a written due bill when:**
- Single, clearly cosmetic issue (one paint inclusion, one piece of trim).
- Software/menu issues — these are always post-delivery fixes anyway.
- Missing accessories (charge cable, key cards, floor mats) — service centers ship these reliably.
- Alignment items service can adjust without bodywork (door alignment within a few mm, wheel alignment).

### Honest counterpoint

Refusal is not free. Multiple TMC posters who refused report VIN reassignment delays of days to weeks, and one ("Refused Delivery Today" — https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/refused-delivery-today.196258/) describes refusing for relatively minor scuffs and a stuck rear-seat-fold button, which a service appointment could likely have resolved. Forum etiquette generally pushes toward refusal, but the regret threads are quieter and harder to find — selection bias is real. Tell the reader: refusal is the strong move when the defect list is long or structural; for a single cosmetic issue, a written due bill plus same-week service often gets the same outcome with less pain.

### Common misunderstandings to debunk

- **The window does not bar all warranty claims.** Mechanical and electrical defects are covered under the basic vehicle warranty regardless of the 100-mile/24-hour window. The window is specifically about cosmetic items that Tesla otherwise classifies as "wear and tear" — chips, scratches, paint inclusions, scuffed trim. Source: TMC "72 hours to report issues" and "3 days / 100 miles" threads above.
- **The window is not your only chance to document.** Photos taken before the window expires are admissible in any later dispute, even if Tesla refuses to schedule service inside it.
- **The clock starts at delivery, not at first drive.** Owners report Tesla treating the timestamp on the app's "Accept Delivery" tap as the start.

## Topic 3: Due bills

### What a due bill is

A due bill is a written record — usually emailed or scanned — that lists specific defects Tesla has agreed to fix after you take delivery. It functions as Tesla's promise that the listed items are not "wear and tear" and will be repaired under warranty when you bring the car in. It is **not** a contract of repair; it is a documentation artifact that prevents service from later claiming the damage is yours.

### What it covers and doesn't

**Covers:** anything visible and documented at delivery — paint chips, scratches, trim alignment, missing accessories, scuffed wheels, interior blemishes.

**Doesn't cover:**
- Issues Tesla deems "within spec" at the service appointment (panel gaps under their internal tolerance, paint blemishes below a size threshold).
- Anything not listed on the due bill — owners report repeatedly that "we'll add that too" verbal promises do not survive a service-advisor handoff.
- Mechanical issues discovered later — those go through normal warranty, not the due bill.

### How to get one in writing

Forum advice converges:
1. List every defect on paper at the lot, before signing.
2. Ask the delivery advisor to add each one to the due bill in the system, then email you a copy from their Tesla email address before you drive away.
3. If they will only verbally promise, do not accept — either refuse delivery or photograph everything and email the advisor and the local service center yourself the same day, with the email subject line stating it is a due-bill request.

## Topic 4: Escalation paths

### Advisor at the lot

Has the most leverage at the moment of delivery: can add items to a due bill, can hold the car back, can escalate to a delivery manager on the spot. Loses almost all leverage once you sign and drive away.

### Local service center

The next stop. They schedule the actual repairs. Forum reports indicate service advisors are the gatekeepers who will say "in spec" or "wear and tear" if a due bill isn't airtight. Citations:
- TMC, "Tesla Service - Post Delivery": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/tesla-service-post-delivery.197586/
- TMC, "Can Tesla mobile service fix this panel gap?": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/can-tesla-mobile-service-fix-this-panel-gap.268256/
- TOO, "Time limit on fixing panel gaps?": https://www.teslaownersonline.com/threads/time-limit-on-fixing-panel-gaps.7051/

A common pattern: try a second service center if the first refuses. Owners on TMC report different verdicts on the same defect at different SCs.

### Tesla support (phone, chat, app)

Useful for paper-trail purposes and for re-routing when a service center has declined. Less useful as a decision-maker — phone/chat support typically cannot override a service advisor's "in spec" call.

### Executive escalation

Tesla's app has an "Escalate this concern for executive review" feature on the support form. Forum sentiment on it is mixed at best.

- TMC, "How to escalate an issue to Tesla's executive team?": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/how-to-escalate-an-issue-to-teslas-executive-team.95289/

### Realistic timelines

- Due bill written, mobile-service items: 1–2 weeks typical.
- Service-center bodywork (paint, alignment): 2–6 weeks, with in-and-out cycles common.
- Executive escalation response: forum reports range from 24 hours to never. Treat it as a low-probability escalation tool, not a guaranteed channel.

## Topic 5: Documentation tips

### Photos

- Shoot in daylight or under bright, even artificial light. Showroom lighting hides paint defects; sun reveals them.
- For each defect: one wide shot showing the panel, one medium shot showing the defect in context with a finger or a key as scale, one close-up.
- Walk the car and shoot every panel even when you don't see anything — the photos establish what was not damaged at delivery, which matters as much as what was.
- Record a continuous video walkaround at the lot before signing. Cybertruck owners on CTOC ("Found damage right after delivery": https://www.cybertruckownersclub.com/forum/threads/found-damage-right-after-delivery.19578/) cite this as the single highest-leverage move for later disputes.

### Written records

- Time-stamped notes app entries for every conversation: who said what, when. The advisor's name and the time of the verbal promise.
- A single dated email to yourself summarizing the day creates an admissible timeline.
- Save the due bill as a PDF and email it to a personal address, not just the Tesla account.

### App vs paper trail

Tesla's official path is the Tesla app's Service section. Use it — it creates a Tesla-side record. But also email the delivery advisor and local service center with the same content. The app's records have been reported lost or overwritten on TMC ("Issues reporting delivery defects via the app": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/issues-reporting-delivery-defects-via-the-app.168281/); the email is your backup.

## Topic 6: When a defect is "live with it"

Honest framing: not every defect is going to get fixed, and some shouldn't be. Setting that expectation up front respects the reader.

### Routinely fixed without a fight

- Software anomalies and menu glitches — almost always fixed by an OTA or a quick service appointment.
- Missing accessories (charger cable, key cards, floor mats) — service centers ship these.
- Wheel alignment, steering wheel off-center.
- Door and frunk alignment when clearly out — service can adjust.
- Single discrete paint chips from transit (when documented at delivery and added to a due bill).

### Routinely refused as "in spec"

- Panel gaps within Tesla's internal tolerance. Forum measurements suggest the threshold sits in the low single-digit millimeters; gaps up to roughly 4 mm are commonly called acceptable. The writer should say "Tesla's published tolerances are not public; owners report service centers calling gaps under roughly 4 mm in spec." Cite:
  - TMC, "Model Y delivery panel gaps": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/model-y-delivery-panel-gaps.341657/
  - TMC, "Panel Gap - Is this within spec?": https://teslamotorsclub.com/tmc/threads/panel-gap-is-this-within-spec.244814/
- Tiny paint inclusions and orange peel — often refused unless above an unstated size threshold.
- Headlight condensation — Tesla's stance is this is normal venting behavior unless water pools.
- Faint interior trim grain mismatches between panels.

### Cosmetic vs functional vs structural

- **Cosmetic** (paint, trim, alignment) — due-bill territory, time-windowed.
- **Functional** (button doesn't work, sensor faults, screen glitches) — covered by basic vehicle warranty regardless of window. The article should explicitly tell the reader this distinction so they don't waste energy fighting for cosmetic-window treatment of a warranty item.
- **Structural** (frame, suspension, glass, airbags) — refuse delivery; do not negotiate.
